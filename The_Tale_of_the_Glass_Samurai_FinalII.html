A Chronicle of Depth & Deflection
The Tale of the
Glass Samurai
Being the True and Mostly Faithful Account
of an Executor Who Ought Not Have Survived
"He bore no shield, wore no helm, and supped poison from his own relics. The scholars named him mad. The Nightlord named him nothing, for the dead keep no counsel."

— Unsigned entry, Roundtable Annals
Chapter I
The Madman's Arsenal

There are warriors who march to battle with forethought. There are warriors who march to battle with prayer. And then there was the Executor, who marched to battle with a manifesto of contradictions strapped to his soul and a grin that suggested he had read the ancient texts in the wrong order and preferred them that way.

He was, by all scholarly reckoning, a walking catastrophe. His armour—fused to his body by some primordial smithcraft no forgemaster would claim—bore the quiet shimmer of glass. Not figurative glass. True, proper, this-shall-shatter-if-the-wind-so-much-as-glances-at-it glass. The Roundtable scholars had examined his relic configuration and produced a twelve-page treatise concluding, in the most cautious academic phrasing available, that he was going to perish.

The trouble began—as trouble ever does—with ambition. The Executor had bound himself to six relics. Three were common, if peculiar: one that filled his veins with flame at the mere commencement of an expedition, one that sharpened every blow by some invisible calculus, and a third whose purpose even he had forgotten, its inscription worn to a single mark. Between those ordinary stones sat two relics of singular manufacture—twin works known to the initiated as the Will of the Balancers—which honed both the melee stroke and the weapon art alike, and whispered of a strange nullification to those whose guard decayed.

The remaining three were Deep Relics, drawn from the abyssal markets, and each was branded with a curse in crimson lettering that would make a prudent man weep. Diminished Faith and Might. Very well. Guard withers upon drinking of the Flask. Meaning every draught of healing thinned his armour like parchment left in rain. Blood betrays its vessel upon taking harm. The Scarlet Rot gnaws when vitality falters. Frost gathers in the bones upon each wound. As though the cosmos had examined his preparations and whispered: more.

But buried within this chorus of self-destruction was a mechanism so elegant that no scholar, in all their pages of analysis, had perceived it. Every curse that stripped his defence fed a single enchantment upon the Balancer relics—unassuming as a dagger concealed within a bouquet:

Attacks are occasionally nullified when guard is weakened.

The more his armour cracked, the more it refused to shatter. Every flask that diminished him roused the nullification. Every curse that gnawed at his constitution fed the very ward that kept him standing. It was the logical equivalent of erecting a house of parchment within a gale and having the gale hold it together.

And then there was the sword.

The Cursed Blade—and it must be noted, he bore this wretched instrument everywhere the way a man bears a keepsake from a departed father, except the keepsake did not drink his lifeblood—required no mine, no forge, no smithing. It was his starting armament, bound to a relic, ready from the moment the expedition commenced. Whilst other Nightfarers spent precious minutes hammering ore in caverns, supplicating the heavens for a worthy drop, the Executor simply walked forth. The sword was always ready, for it was always hungry, and hunger requires no preparation.

The scholars, upon finally comprehending his full array, appended a thirteenth page to their treatise. It contained a single word, underscored thrice: Why?

The Executor, had he been asked, would have answered with rare honesty. He had assembled this configuration as a jest. A passing fancy. A brief sojourn to the training grounds to observe the damage figures perform amusing feats before returning to his proper, balanced, sensible preparations.

He did not return.

Chapter II
Ankles of the Colossus

He descended alone into the Deep of Night, Depth Two, where the air tasted of iron and the darkness possessed weight. No companions. No reprieve. Merely a man, a cursed sword, and the unshakeable conviction that the arithmetic would prove favourable.

The Guardian Golem stood at the clearing's edge like a monument to everything the Executor was not: immense, stable, constructed with structural integrity. Its stone limbs groaned as it turned to face the intruder, each stride sending tremors through the earth.

The Executor regarded it. He regarded the enormous stone legs. He regarded the ankles.

A smile crossed his face—the particular smile of a man who has identified a weakness so fundamental, so embarrassingly architectural, that the only reasonable response is violence applied at precisely the correct height.

He went low. The cursed blade bit into stone where it met the earth, again and again, a rhythm so swift and exact that sparks flew like a forge in full temper. The Golem swung overhead. The Executor did not look up. He had no need. He was beneath the operational reach of the creature's arms, crouched like a man polishing boots whilst the house blazed above him. Each strike fed the invisible calculus of his relics—melee potency compounding upon physical might compounding upon weapon art fury—and the ankles, those proud, weight-bearing ankles, began to fracture.

And then the art gauge filled.

Where he had stood—small, mortal, frothing with the quiet madness of a man hacking a colossus at knee-height—there now erupted a Crucible Beast. Wings of amber radiance. Horns of primordial wrath. A creature torn from the earth's memory of what violence meant before civilisation taught it restraint. The Golem found itself face-to-face with something whose horns exceeded the span of its torso.

The impact was not a battle. It was a demolition. Stone exploded outward in a halo of ruin. Limbs that had walked for centuries scattered across the clearing like dice cast by a particularly wrathful god.

Three minutes and eighteen seconds. The expedition clock had scarcely begun its count.

Chapter III
The Giants' Fortification

Between the golem and the night stood the Castle—that looming bulk of stone and misery that squatted upon the centre of Limveld like a toad upon a throne it had not earned. Every Nightfarer knew the Castle. Most approached it with caution, a plan, and at minimum two companions to share the suffering.

On this day, the Castle had Trolls.

Four of them. One upon each rampart, each the height of a minor tower, each possessed of a talent so specific and so infuriating that it warranted its own entry in the Roundtable bestiary: they threw pots. Enormous, enchanted, incandescent vessels of alchemical fury, hurled with the precision of a court bowler who had devoted his entire existence to the singular art of striking a man between the eyes from two hundred paces.

The scholarly consensus was: do not engage the Trolls alone. The scholarly consensus was, by this point in the expedition, a document the Executor had never read.

He approached from the northern stair, where the first Troll stood apart from its brethren's line of sight. This was—by the Executor's own admission—the lone moment of tactical prudence in an otherwise strategically deranged expedition. The Troll spotted him. A pot sailed through the air, trailing fire like a comet with poor aim. The Executor sidestepped, closed the distance, and introduced the cursed blade to the Troll's ankles with the familiarity of a man who had, not thirty minutes prior, perfected this very technique upon a stone golem.

The first Troll fell. A Dormant Power bloomed from its remains. The Executor collected it and turned toward the second.

The second Troll was not alone. Exile soldiers crowded its feet like worshippers at the base of an exceedingly violent idol. Worse, the third Troll across the ramparts had taken notice. Two giants now lobbed their alchemical ordinance in overlapping arcs, and the flames they left upon the stone did not merely burn—they lingered, like grudges held by persons with nothing better to occupy their time.

The Executor fought through it. Deflect a soldier. Dodge a pot. Strike the ankle. Dodge another pot. Drink a flask—feel the armour thin from the curse—feel the nullification rise in response. The loop. The mad, beautiful, self-devouring loop. The second Troll buckled and fell.

And then the rain came.

Not rain of water. Rain of night. The tide was closing, the azure fire sweeping inward across the map, and the remaining Trolls upon their perches were now behind the advancing wall of cerulean flame. The Executor regarded the encroaching destruction, regarded the two remaining giants still hurling pots from within the burning periphery, and made the only sensible decision available to a man who had long since abandoned sensibility.

He left them. Two Trolls slain, two forsaken to the tide. There was, after all, a war still to fight, and the night was not going to wait for a man to finish his quarrel with masonry-dwelling pot enthusiasts.

Chapter IV
The Horsemen Who Charged

Night fell with the certainty of a headsman's axe, and with it came the cavalries.

They arrived in tandem—two riders clad in pitch-black armour thinly painted with dried blood, mounted upon funeral steeds whose hooves made no honest sound upon the earth. They were knights of the Fell Omen's old retinue, once deliverers of death to great warriors and champions, now condemned to ride the dim roads of the Deep for all eternity. They wore no heraldry. They bore no banner. Their black hair streamed behind them like the memory of a funeral that had never quite ended.

The first bore a glaive—a jet-black halberd with a blade as weighty as a bludgeon, capable of sweeping attacks that covered half the field. The second carried a flail—three spiked heads of iron chained to a haft, swung with the lazy menace of someone who had never once lost a duel and saw no reason to begin losing now.

Together, they were a Night Boss fashioned for parties. Coordinated trios would divide the duo—one rider per pair, controlled positioning, the whole tedious choreography of survival.

The Executor was alone. He had no choreography. He had a cursed sword and a hypothesis that had served him well against a golem several hours prior: that which charges may be deflected.

The glaive-rider lowered its weapon and surged forth. The earth screamed beneath hooves that had borne the dead. The Executor planted his feet, raised the cursed blade flat before him, and—at the precise instant the glaive kissed the edge of his guard—deflected.

The sound was remarkable. A crystalline ring that echoed across the field like a bell recast in the same breath it shattered. The glaive snapped aside. The funeral steed buckled. And the rider—that ancient, blood-painted knight who had charged through uncountable nights—was thrown from its mount like a drunkard ejected from a tavern for unpaid debts.

It landed hard. Black armour clanged against earth. And before it could rise—before it could recall that it was supposed to be immortal and terrifying—the Executor was upon it. The cursed blade found the seam between plate and purpose, drove through the rider's torso where it lay splayed upon the ground, and the critical strike that followed produced a figure so vast it flickered twice, as though the world itself were verifying the arithmetic.

The first rider dissolved into nothing. The entire exchange had lasted seconds. Not many of them.

The flail-bearer had witnessed all of this. It was dismounted by the same method—the deflection, the ring, the fall. But this one rose.

What followed was a proper duel. One-on-one. A man with a cursed sword and a spectre of the Fell Omen's guard, both afoot, both at close quarters, the triple flail whistling through the dark in great spiralling arcs. The Executor stepped inside the reach. The cursed blade answered. The knight swung again. The Executor deflected. The knight staggered. The blade answered once more. It was, in its fashion, almost courteous—the sort of contest bards would set to verse, had the bards not all been devoured by Tricephalos during the previous expedition.

It lasted less than a minute.

Chapter V
The Hunt of Three Wolves

The second day dawned with the taste of ash and a proclamation that no solitary warrior ever wishes to behold:

Fire-summoning beasts have appeared.

The Tricephalos raid. Three Sundered Tricephalos—great snarling wolves torn from the body of the Nightlord Gladius itself—scattered across the map in three separate dominions, each ringed in crimson upon the world chart like wounds upon a body. Between them, spirit wolves spawned ceaselessly, and a scorching affliction gnawed at vitality like a slow tithe upon the audacity of remaining alive.

Most companies divide this labour. One warrior per wolf. Coordination, communication, signals upon the map. Orderly. Efficient. Fashioned for three.

The Executor was one.

He found the first wolf at the sixteenth minute. The cursed blade sang its hungry song. The beast, unprepared for the sheer discourtesy of a man who did not pause to assess the situation, fell in a tempest of deflections and counters. Upon dying, it spawned three lesser fire wolves. They gave chase. The Executor did not cease running.

The second wolf was stronger—the raid's cruel arithmetic ensured that each subsequent beast absorbed the fury of its predecessor. The fire wolves from the first slaying had followed, joining the second wolf's retinue. The scorching tithe intensified. The arena became a furnace.

The third wolf was a war unto itself. Every creature that had perished before now served as fuel for this final beast. It stood flanked by packs of spectral fire wolves, every one of them howling, every one of them ablaze, every one of them personally invested in ending the Executor's improbable career. The Sundered Tricephalos pounced. The Executor deflected it. The ring was louder now—louder than it had been for the cavalries, louder than it had been for the golem. It was the sound of a man who had deflected so many things in one expedition that the act had ceased being a technique and become a temperament.

The wolf perished at twenty-eight minutes and fifteen seconds. The flames extinguished. The fire wolves dissolved like ill dreams at dawn.

He had perished thrice during the hunt. Once to the second Tricephalos. Once to a nameless creature in the chaos. Once to the environment—a fall from a ledge, of all things, the most ignoble death available to a man who had just bested a trio of fire-born god-beasts. He chose never to speak of the fall. Not then. Not ever.

Beast's Hunt acquired. Successive strikes restore vigour. Vigour recovery hastened.

A boon fashioned for companies. Claimed by one man. Whilst aflame.

Chapter VI
Four Seconds and a Hippo

Night fell for the second time, and with it came a dual summons: the Crucible Knight, and the Golden Hippopotamus.

This was a contest fashioned as a crescendo. The Crucible Knight arrived first—that ancient armoured pilgrim of horn and tail, whose slow, deliberate strokes bore the weight of theological certainty. At half vitality, it would roar, holy light would burst from its frame, and the Golden Hippopotamus would lumber forth to join the fray. Two foes. Simultaneously. In an arena constructed for coordinated trios.

The Executor met the Crucible Knight at the thirty-first minute and thirty-eighth second. The knight raised its colossal blade. The Executor deflected. And again. Each parry charged the Suncatcher, each charge pushing the blade's accumulated fury closer to critical mass.

The opening arrived. The knight committed to a heavy overhead—slow, proclaimed, dripping with intent—and the Executor stepped within the arc, drove the cursed blade upward, and unleashed the critical strike.

Twenty-five hundred. The Crucible Knight—which had entered this contest with the serene assurance of a being that has existed since before geometry was conceived—simply ceased to be.

The Golden Hippopotamus had barely crossed the threshold. It arrived at a gallop, jaws agape, prepared to contribute to what was meant to be a devastating assault, and instead found itself alone in a chamber with a man who ought not to have been producing figures that large. The hippo charged—for of course it charged; all things charged—and the Executor deflected it as well, because deflecting that which charged was no longer a stratagem. It was a vocation.

Thirty-one minutes and thirty-eight seconds to thirty-one minutes and forty-two seconds. The entire dual encounter—Crucible Knight and Golden Hippopotamus, a trial meant to test the coordination of a full company—resolved in four heartbeats. The flask charge materialised atop the hippo's cooling remains like a receipt for a transaction the Executor had not anticipated completing this swiftly.

The relics—those mad, contradictory, self-devouring relics—were compounding upon one another like coin accruing interest in a treasury managed by a particularly zealous steward. Physical upon melee upon weapon art upon the sheer effrontery of a man who had transmuted his own curses into armour. And each Balancer nullification that flickered across his body was a reminder that the arithmetic was not merely functioning; it was boasting.

Chapter VII
The Gaping Jaw

The Final Day arrived as Final Days must: with too much silence, too little vitality, and the distinct awareness that something colossal was breathing just beyond the veil of sight.

Adel, Baron of Night—the Gaping Jaw—awaited him at the terminus of all things. A Nightlord. A monstrous black drake with a crooked frame capable of harbouring lightning, its defining feature not a weapon, nor wings, but a maw. A sideways jaw, vast and gnarled, lined with grinding teeth fashioned to devour anything and everything it encountered. Once regal and unrivalled atop its perch, it had been consumed by the Night's Tide—starved of prey until hunger became madness, and madness became appetite without limit.

The arena was wide and barren. The Executor stood at its edge, cursed blade drawn, his glass armour shimmering with all the resilience of a chandelier in an earthquake. His flask count ran low. His resurrections were spent.

Adel charged.

The Executor had awaited this. Every foe in this expedition had instructed him in the same lesson: the wolf that lunged, the golem that stomped, the Trolls that hurled, the cavalries that galloped, the Tricephalos that pounced, the Crucible Knight that swung, the hippo that rushed. All things charge. All things are deflected. He had elevated this observation into a universal law, tested it against every adversary the Deep could offer, and found it wanting for nothing.

The deflection was clean. Adel's charge crashed against the cursed blade with the sound of a continent splitting. The guard counter followed. The Suncatcher blazed. Figures floated upward into the dark like lanterns released at a funeral—except the funeral was the Baron's.

And then Adel seized him.

The sideways jaws closed. The Executor vanished within the maw of the Nightlord, and for a terrible, airless moment, the world reduced itself to darkness and the sound of grinding teeth against glass armour. The grab—Adel's signature, the assault that scholars devote entire chapters to avoiding—had connected. The Baron chewed. Damage erupted in crimson. Vitality plummeted. Blood loss built with savage swiftness.

The Executor survived. By what margin, he did not care to calculate. He was expelled, shattered and furious, and before the jaws could close again the cursed blade found its mark. A critical strike. Through the Nightlord's gaping throat. Twenty-five hundred. The Baron staggered backward, roaring, violet lightning crackling across its crooked frame.

Another critical. Same wound. The cursed blade buried itself in the Baron's jaw and twisted.

But Adel did not fall. It swung. It lunged. It snapped. Lightning exploded across the arena in great violet fissures. The Executor's flask count reached zero. His resurrection tally read empty. His vitality was a sliver—a single blow from oblivion, a whisper from the afterlife, a rounding error from the death tally.

He possessed nothing more.

Save the Suncatcher.

The cursed blade hummed. Deflections accumulated in its edge like a storm gathering behind glass—all the charges, all the counters, all the madness of a thirty-six-minute solitary expedition compressed into a single point of pale golden light at the tip of a sword that was never intended for this purpose.

The Executor charged.

Not a dodge. Not a retreat. Not the measured, calculated advance of a warrior managing his reserves. A charge. Across the arena. Directly at the Gaping Jaw. One blow from death, zero flasks, zero resurrections, running at a Nightlord with nothing but a cursed sword and the Suncatcher blazing like a comet forged from poor decisions.

Adel opened its maw.

The Suncatcher connected first.

The light was blinding. Adel, Baron of Night, the Gaping Jaw, Nightlord of the Deep, collapsed mid-lunge, its maw still agape, its teeth still reaching, its vitality emptied by a man wrought of glass and the specific variety of madness that refuses the prudent course even when the prudent course is the only course that could possibly succeed.

Thirty-six minutes. Thirty-two seconds. Five deaths across the whole of the expedition. None upon the final day.

The arena fell silent. The darkness receded. The expedition's completion blazed in golden script across the heavens, and the Executor—battered, bloodied, cursed six ways unto damnation, his armour cracked in more places than it was whole—rose to his full height.

He struck a pose.

Not a victory salute. Not a humble bow. A pose. Shoulders thrown back, chin raised, one arm extended at an angle so precise it could only have been rehearsed before a looking glass in a prior life. Those who recognised the stance—and there were none present to recognise it, for he was alone, but the cosmos recognised it—would have named it the posture of a man channelling something ancient, something theatrical, something profoundly Eastern and deeply concerned with the art of standing dramatically whilst the world collapses around you.

The vision began to darken. The firmament swelled with distant music. And the Glass Samurai held the pose—held it through the fade, held it through the silence, held it through eternity—as though the pose were the true victory, and the Nightlord merely the occasion for it.

Epilogue
The Jest That Bit Back

The most perilous detail of this chronicle—the one the Roundtable scholars shall debate for generations, the one that shall keep them awake long past the hour when sensible folk cease quarrelling over relic synergies—is that the Glass Samurai was never intended to function.

He had assembled the configuration as a jest. Let me merely observe the damage figures in the training grounds, he had told himself, in the manner of a man who tells himself he shall have but a single drink. The curses were too numerous, the penalties too severe, the absence of healing relics a gap so wide one might drive a Tricephalos through it.

And the configuration had responded by conquering Depth Two on its maiden voyage.

The terrible truth—the one that made the other Nightfarers regard their own meticulously-balanced, exhaustively-optimised relic sets with sudden suspicion—was this: the configuration was not yet complete. The deep relics could be mightier. The curses, better chosen. Frost, bleed, madness, slumber—the afflictions that fed the Executor's ancient passive like kindling to a furnace—had scarcely been explored. The Glass Samurai, in his present form, was a rough draught.

A rough draught that had slain a Nightlord whilst posing.

There is one final matter. A detail that appears in no battle record, no scholarly treatise, no testimony offered by the Executor himself. It is this:

The cursed blade had been silent when he first bore it. A relic among relics, bound to a slot, granted without ceremony. But somewhere during the expedition—perhaps during the golem's demolition, perhaps when the cavalries fell, perhaps in the breath between the second Tricephalos and the third—it had begun to hum.

A low sound. Nigh imperceptible. The sort of sound a blade makes when it is not merely being wielded, but gratified.

The Executor had noticed. He had elected not to mention it. He had elected not to mention, either, that the cursed blade felt lighter now than when the expedition commenced—that the hunger which had always defined it, that slow draining of his vitality, had shifted in character from parasitic to symbiotic. As though the blade had found, after centuries of being borne by men who feared it, a host who comprehended what it truly desired.

Not to be carried. To be unleashed.

The scholars' treatise would eventually be expanded to fourteen pages, the final addition a footnote so small that only the most attentive readers would catch it:

"Addendum: we can no longer ascertain, with certainty,
whether the Executor wields the sword,
or whether the sword wields the Executor.
We recommend further inquiry.
We do not recommend proximity."

The Executor was last observed departing the Shrouded Roundtable Hold at an hour when all other Nightfarers had retired to rest. He walked without haste. The cursed blade lay across his shoulders. His glass armour caught the firelight and fractured it into hues that ought not to have existed in a place this dark.

He was humming the same note as the sword.

He did not look back.
